Choosing a stroller is one of the biggest gear decisions new parents face. You will likely use your stroller nearly every day for two to four years, so getting the right one matters. With hundreds of models on the market ranging from under $100 to well over $1,500, the options can feel overwhelming.

This comprehensive guide breaks down every stroller type, the features that actually matter, and which stroller fits your lifestyle and budget. Whether you are a city dweller navigating narrow sidewalks, a suburban parent loading up at the mall, or an avid runner who wants baby along for the ride, there is a stroller built for you.

## Types of Baby Strollers

### Full-Size Strollers

Full-size strollers are the workhorses of the stroller world. They offer the most features, the smoothest ride, and the best storage capacity. Most full-size strollers accommodate children from birth (with a flat recline or infant car seat adapter) through 50 pounds or more.

**Pros:**
- Generous storage baskets
- Full recline for napping
- Robust suspension for a smooth ride
- Adjustable handlebars on many models
- Compatible with infant car seat adapters

**Cons:**
- Heavier, typically 20 to 30 pounds
- Bulkier fold
- May not fit easily in smaller car trunks

Popular full-size options in 2026 include the UPPAbaby VISTA V3, Bugaboo Fox 5, and Nuna MIXX Next.

### Lightweight and Umbrella Strollers

Lightweight strollers weigh under 15 pounds and fold compactly, making them ideal for travel and quick errands. Umbrella strollers are the most basic version, with a simple frame and minimal padding. More premium lightweight strollers offer surprising comfort and features despite their slim profiles.

**Pros:**
- Easy to carry and transport
- Compact fold fits in overhead bins and small trunks
- Quick to set up and break down
- Affordable options available

**Cons:**
- Less cushioning and suspension
- Smaller canopies
- Limited storage
- Most suitable for babies who can sit unassisted (around 6 months)

### Jogging Strollers

Jogging strollers feature three large air-filled tires, a fixed or lockable front wheel, and a hand brake. They are engineered for stability at speed and designed to absorb the impact of running on various surfaces.

**Pros:**
- Excellent suspension and shock absorption
- Large tires handle trails, gravel, and uneven terrain
- Hand brake for downhill control
- Many models have high weight limits (up to 75 pounds)

**Cons:**
- Heavier and bulkier than standard strollers
- Not ideal for tight indoor spaces
- Most manufacturers recommend waiting until baby is 6 to 8 months old before running (always check your specific model)
- Higher price point

**Safety note:** The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) recommends that parents do not jog with a baby until the child has adequate neck and head control. Always use the wrist safety strap while running.

### Travel Systems

A travel system bundles a stroller frame with a matching infant car seat and base. The car seat clicks directly into the stroller, allowing you to move a sleeping baby from car to stroller without disturbing them.

**Pros:**
- Seamless car-to-stroller transition
- Cost savings compared to buying separately
- Guaranteed compatibility between seat and stroller
- Ideal for newborns

**Cons:**
- The infant car seat is outgrown by 12 to 15 months
- Can be heavy when car seat is attached
- May be less versatile than mixing and matching brands

### Double Strollers

Double strollers come in two main configurations:

- **Tandem (front-to-back):** Narrower footprint that fits through standard doorways. One child sits in front, the other behind.
- **Side-by-side:** Both children have equal views and space, but the stroller is wider and may not fit through all doorways.

For families with a toddler and a newborn, look for double strollers that accept an infant car seat in one position while the older child uses the regular seat.

## Key Features to Consider

### Weight and Portability

Stroller weight matters more than most parents realize before purchase. You will lift this stroller in and out of your car trunk, carry it up stairs, and maneuver it through crowded spaces.

| Stroller Type | Typical Weight | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Umbrella | 8 to 12 lbs | Travel, quick errands |
| Lightweight | 12 to 18 lbs | Daily use with portability |
| Full-size | 20 to 30 lbs | Primary everyday stroller |
| Jogging | 25 to 35 lbs | Running and active lifestyles |
| Double | 28 to 40 lbs | Two children |

### Fold Type

Stroller folds fall into two categories:

- **One-hand fold:** Essential for parents who need to fold the stroller while holding a child. Many modern strollers offer a standing fold, meaning the stroller stays upright when folded.
- **Two-hand fold:** Typically produces a more compact folded size but requires both hands and sometimes a foot to operate.

Test the fold mechanism before purchasing. A stroller with a frustrating fold will become a daily annoyance.

### Suspension and Wheels

Suspension determines how smooth the ride feels for your baby. There are three main wheel types:

- **Foam-filled wheels:** Maintenance-free, moderate shock absorption, standard on most strollers.
- **Air-filled (pneumatic) tires:** Best shock absorption, handles rough terrain, but can get flat.
- **Rubber wheels:** Durable, good traction, common on mid-range strollers.

For rough sidewalks, gravel paths, or cobblestones, prioritize larger wheels and robust suspension. For smooth mall floors and paved paths, smaller wheels with swivel capability offer better maneuverability.

### Storage

An accessible, generously sized storage basket makes a real difference in daily use. Consider:

- Can you reach the basket without tipping the stroller?
- Is the basket opening wide enough to fit a diaper bag?
- Are there parent pockets or cup holders included?
- Does the stroller have a snack tray or child cup holder?

### Canopy

A good canopy protects your baby from sun, wind, and light rain. Look for:

- UPF 50+ sun protection
- Extendable panels with a peek-a-boo window
- Ventilation panels for hot weather
- Magnetic closures on peek-a-boo windows (quieter than Velcro)

## Safety Standards for Strollers

All strollers sold in the United States must meet the ASTM F833 safety standard, enforced by the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC). When shopping, verify:

- The stroller has a five-point harness
- Brakes lock securely on both rear wheels
- There are no sharp edges or pinch points in the folding mechanism
- The stroller has a JPMA (Juvenile Products Manufacturers Association) certification seal
- There are no active recalls (check CPSC.gov)

Always register your stroller with the manufacturer to receive recall notifications.

## Best Stroller by Lifestyle

### City Living

City parents need a stroller that is narrow enough for crowded sidewalks, light enough to carry up subway stairs, and compact enough for apartment storage.

**Priorities:**
- Width under 22 inches
- One-hand fold with standing fold capability
- Lightweight (under 20 pounds)
- Good maneuverability with swivel front wheels
- Compact folded dimensions

### Suburban Lifestyle

Suburban parents typically drive to destinations and need a stroller that loads easily into a car trunk, handles mall floors and park paths equally well, and offers generous storage for shopping.

**Priorities:**
- Large storage basket
- Easy car trunk loading
- Smooth ride on varied surfaces
- Cup holders and parent organizer
- Compatibility with infant car seat

### Active and Outdoor Lifestyle

Active parents who run, hike, or spend significant time on trails need specialized strollers built for performance.

**Priorities:**
- Air-filled tires with suspension
- Hand brake and wrist strap
- High weight limit for longevity
- Weather protection (rain cover, full canopy)
- Durable frame that handles rough terrain

## Budget Ranges

### Budget-Friendly: Under $200

At this price point, you can find reliable lightweight strollers and basic full-size options. Brands like Graco, Chicco, and Kolcraft offer well-reviewed options under $200. You may sacrifice premium suspension, one-hand folds, and high-end fabrics, but safety and basic functionality remain strong.

### Mid-Range: $200 to $600

This range offers the best value for most families. Strollers in this bracket often include features like one-hand folds, adjustable handlebars, all-wheel suspension, and generous canopies. Look at brands like Baby Jogger, Chicco Bravo, and Graco Premier.

### Premium: $600 to $1,000

Premium strollers deliver top-tier materials, advanced suspension, modular configurations, and sleek design. Brands like UPPAbaby, Nuna, and Cybex dominate this range.

### Luxury: Over $1,000

Luxury strollers from brands like Bugaboo, Stokke, and Silver Cross offer the finest materials, innovative features, and distinctive styling. These strollers often hold their resale value well.

| Budget Range | What You Get | Example Brands |
|---|---|---|
| Under $200 | Basic features, reliable safety | Graco, Chicco, Kolcraft |
| $200 - $600 | Great features, good build quality | Baby Jogger, Chicco Bravo |
| $600 - $1,000 | Premium materials, advanced features | UPPAbaby, Nuna, Cybex |
| Over $1,000 | Luxury design, top-tier performance | Bugaboo, Stokke, Silver Cross |

## When to Transition Stroller Types

Many families end up owning two strollers: a primary stroller and a travel or lightweight stroller. Here is a general timeline:

- **Birth to 6 months:** Travel system or full-size stroller with infant car seat adapter. Baby needs a full recline position.
- **6 to 12 months:** Baby can sit in a standard stroller seat. Consider transitioning out of the infant car seat in the stroller.
- **12 to 24 months:** A lightweight stroller becomes increasingly useful for travel and quick outings.
- **24 to 36 months:** Many children alternate between walking and riding. An ultralight umbrella stroller works well for backup.
- **3 to 4 years:** Most children phase out of strollers, though a lightweight option can still help during long outings, travel, or nap times.

## Stroller Accessories Worth Buying

Not all accessories are created equal. Here are the ones that genuinely improve your stroller experience:

- **Rain cover:** A must-have for any climate. Universal covers are available, but brand-specific covers fit better.
- **Mosquito net:** Essential for summer walks and outdoor dining.
- **Cup holder:** If your stroller does not include one, an aftermarket cup holder is inexpensive and invaluable.
- **Stroller organizer:** A compact bag that attaches to the handlebar for your phone, keys, and wallet.
- **Travel bag:** Protects your stroller during flights. Padded bags are worth the investment for expensive strollers.
- **Footmuff:** For cold-weather climates, a stroller footmuff keeps baby warm without bulky blankets.
- **Board attachment:** For older siblings who are tired of walking, a ride-along board lets them stand on the back of the stroller.

**Accessories to skip:**
- Handlebar-mounted fans (safety concern if they fall)
- Heavy aftermarket trays that affect stroller balance
- Excessive handlebar weight (can cause tipping when baby is not in the seat)

## Tips for Stroller Shopping

1. **Test the fold in the store.** Fold and unfold the stroller yourself before buying. If it frustrates you in the store, it will frustrate you daily.
2. **Check your car trunk.** Measure your trunk space and compare it to the stroller's folded dimensions.
3. **Push it around.** Steer with one hand, navigate tight turns, and test the brake.
4. **Consider resale value.** Premium brands like UPPAbaby and Bugaboo retain 40 to 60 percent of their value on resale markets.
5. **Wait for sales.** Major stroller sales happen during Black Friday, Amazon Prime Day, and when new model years launch.
6. **Read recent reviews.** Stroller models change yearly. Look for reviews of the specific model year you are purchasing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What stroller is best for a newborn?

A travel system or full-size stroller with a full recline position is best for newborns. Newborns cannot support their own heads, so they need to lie flat or be secured in an infant car seat that clicks into the stroller frame. Avoid upright-only strollers until your baby can sit unassisted, typically around 6 months.

### How much should I spend on a stroller?

Most families find excellent value in the $200 to $600 range. Strollers in this bracket offer solid safety, good build quality, and useful features like one-hand folds and adjustable handlebars. Spending more gets you premium materials and advanced features, but safety standards are consistent across all price points.

### Can I use a jogging stroller as my everyday stroller?

You can, but there are trade-offs. Jogging strollers are heavier, bulkier, and less maneuverable in tight spaces like stores and restaurants. If you plan to run regularly, consider having a jogging stroller for exercise and a lighter stroller for daily errands.

### When should I stop using a stroller?

Most children naturally transition out of strollers between ages 3 and 4. However, a lightweight stroller can remain useful for travel, long outings, and situations where your child may need to nap on the go. There is no strict cutoff, and every child's stamina is different.

### Are expensive strollers worth it?

Expensive strollers typically offer better materials, smoother rides, longer durability, and stronger resale value. If you plan to use the stroller daily for multiple years or across multiple children, a premium stroller can be a worthwhile investment. However, budget-friendly strollers meet the same safety standards and can serve families well.
